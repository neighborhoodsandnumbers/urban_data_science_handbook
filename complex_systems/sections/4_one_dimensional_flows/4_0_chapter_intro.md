# One-Dimensional Flows

*In 1798, Thomas Malthus published An Essay on the Principle of Population and terrified a generation. Population, he argued, grows geometrically while food production grows only arithmetically — famine, disease, and war were not aberrations but mathematical certainties. Four decades later, the Belgian mathematician Pierre-François Verhulst offered a correction as elegant as Malthus's forecast was dire. Population does not grow without bound. It slows as it approaches a carrying capacity — a ceiling set by resources, space, and competition. His equation, $\dot{N} = rN(1 - N/K)$, captured this in a single line: growth proportional to both the current population and the room left to grow. It was published in 1838, promptly forgotten, and not rediscovered for nearly a century.*

*Verhulst's logistic equation is the simplest system in which the geometry of a differential equation — which direction the arrows point on the number line — tells you everything about long-term behavior. It has two fixed points: $N^* = 0$, which is unstable (a small population grows), and $N^* = K$, which is stable (a population near the carrying capacity returns to it). No closed-form solution is needed. The qualitative picture is enough. That geometric perspective — reading dynamics from the shape of $f(x)$ rather than solving $\dot{x} = f(x)$ for $x(t)$ — is what this chapter teaches.*
