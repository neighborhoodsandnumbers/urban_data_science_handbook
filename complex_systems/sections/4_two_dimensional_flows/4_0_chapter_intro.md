# Two-Dimensional Flows

*In 1881, Henri Poincaré began publishing a series of memoirs that would transform the study of differential equations. Working on problems in celestial mechanics — the same three-body problem whose intractability we encountered in Section 1.2 — he realized that most systems of two ODEs cannot be solved in closed form. Rather than conceding defeat, he invented a new approach: study the geometry of solution curves in the plane. He classified the possible fixed-point types (nodes, saddles, spirals, centers), introduced the concept of a limit cycle — an isolated closed orbit that attracts or repels nearby trajectories — and proved the first results about when such cycles must exist. The phase plane was born {cite}`strogatz2015nonlinear`.*

*In Chapter 3, the geometry of $f(x)$ on a line gave us everything: fixed points, stability, basins of attraction. But the line was too simple for oscillation — we proved that 1D flows can only approach equilibria or escape to infinity. The plane changes everything. Trajectories can spiral, cycle, and wind around each other without crossing. This chapter delivers on the promise made in Section 3.1: the richer dynamics that one dimension forbids.*
